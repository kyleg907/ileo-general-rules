2  Eligible LEGO robot kits and parts

Rule Notes/Why

A team's solution to the final project may only use the LEGO parts from one LEGO Robotics kit. The kits typically in use in the ILEO program:

  • LEGO MINDSTORMS NXT Education #9797 (any of the 3 revisions)
  • LEGO MINDSTORMS EV3 Education #45544
  • LEGO SPIKE Prime #45678

Other variations of these kits, and LEGO robot generations exist. The home/retail versions we generally allow. We may limit certain parts in those kits to avoid giving an unfair advantage. You may acquire additional parts to bring retail kits in line with the education kits.

For details, see the 'Kit Compatibility Statement.pdf' in the 'Tips, tools, and hints' folder. In it we explain the differences between the kits and what we will and will not allow at the final competition. It will also explain rules for parts swaps that are allowed between kits. The document can be found in the ILEO box site.

No other non_LEGO style/brand of robot is eligible for competition in our program.

Students might compete against students with different kits. Minimizing those differences keeps competition fair.

Standard kits control costs so the schools.

As always, the education version of the kit is considered to be the gold standard, but some schools have purchased the retail version of the kits in the past and these may be used in the competition, with the restrictions listed in the document.

Lost or broken parts should be replaced, and ideally all LEGO robotic kits given to the students are complete as if new. Parts may be purchased from the LEGO group, other sources, or taken from unused kits. Mentors may remove parts that are too easily lost (small gears), expensive to replace, or are distracting in class (tank treads, rubber bands). But those materials should be made available on team request if they have a legitimate need for the part.

Only parts with a LEGO Group part number that are designed to connect to standard LEGO bricks or LEGO Technic parts (including the wire and battery cover) No bins, lids, manuals, manuals are valid for use.

Said another way, components in the box which are not LEGO pieces (such as the instruction booklet, the part trays, sticker sheet and packaging) are NOT allowed to be used as part of the robot. The intelligent brick, sensors, and wires ARE considered to be LEGO parts. If there is any question either ask a judge at the competition or contact the core team beforehand.

 Creative uses of these parts may be an advantage, disruptive to the competition, or damaging to reusable things in the kit.
Parts of the kit may not be modified or damaged for use in the competition, or in class. Avoid damage to reusable parts of kits for use by future students. Avoid advantage given by non-LEGO parts.

3 Divisions

Rule Notes/Why

ILEO has 3 divisions for students to participate, based on age or skill level:

  • Beginner: Open to elementary and middle school students. We strongly encourage students who have participated in the program in the past move from beginner to standard.
  • Standard: Open to elementary and middle school students.
  • Advanced: Open to all students, this is the only level that high school students can compete at.
    • Advanced is hard. If your students have not been in the ILEO program before, we do not recommend placing them in the Advanced division unless they are very familiar with the LEGO Mindstorms platform and have solid problem-solving skills.
    • If you are not sure, but the students really want to try, have them do the Advanced weekly projects. If they do well there, they will likely be OK with the long-term projects.

We'd like the competition to be even. The number and size of divisions affects the materials the ILEO core team needs to prepare for the competition. It also affects the complexity and timeliness of scoring for the competition.

We expect teachers/mentors/classes to familiarize students with the concepts in the ILEO curriculum documents in the iLEO box folder. Admittedly the documented are tailored toward older students due to the reading level and the amount of reading.

You have flexibility in how to present these materials, especially for younger students. Though typically classes work directly from the documents. We also do not expect you to finish the documents, but you should finish material that covers all of the sensors, or at least the sensors needed for the final project.

The curriculum has the building blocks students will need in preparing their solutions to the final project, this year and beyond. We also hope to teach the students about engineering as a discipline and career.

4 Robot Behavior

Rule Notes/Why

All robots/programs must run autonomously. That is, once you start the program, the robot must complete the task on its own without any human intervention. Touching the robot typically stops the run and scoring will be performed. Certain kinds of interference or project rules might disqualify the run.

For example, you may not maintain a Bluetooth or other connection (long stick) to the robot to communicate with it or

This makes projects easier to solve, so we want everyone to face the same challenges and make the competition fair. We want to encourage creative builds and programming solutions.
Start your program on the robot using buttons or sensors on the robot. Related to the previous rule about autonomous robots. Also, we're uncertain about interference that many Bluetooth connections might cause. It also makes it clearer to the judge when to start timing your run.

No one may interfere with the robots of the teams they are competing against.

Fairness.

If a robot interferes with a robot in another arena by accident in the longer term competition, the just may give the affected team(s) another run. For short term, this depends on the specific project, as it is more common for two robots to be operating near each other.

To save floor space and materials, we generally place 2 arenas together. One 4 foot by 8 foot area typically hosts two teams doing judged runs at the same time. We generally design projects to its unlikely either run will interfere with the other. But robot designs, or a malfunctioning robot that can reach over the 2x4 walls could interfere.

Typically, before a run starts, the robot must fit in the bounds of an 8 1/2 inch by 11 inch (US Letter) piece of paper. Some projects may specify different dimensions. The wires that connect motors and sensors may go beyond the boundaries as tying them down is difficult and may damage the wires. The wires must still fit in the bounds when you press/squish the wires inward toward the robot.

Once the program is started on the robot, the robot may "unfold" itself and become larger than stated dimensions.

 Some of our projects are too easy to solve with a large or wide robot. Unfolding adds a challenge in build in programming to be fair to students with other solutions.

If a project does not include a wall around the area (typically made of 2x4s ), the robot may leave the arena and return to it during a run. However, it should not go far beyond the surface more than a foot, or at the discretion of the judges. Its best to warn the judge and prepare the ground if you plan for your robot to drive around the arena.

If a project has a wall, we do not want robots leaving the arena area enclosed, unless explicitly allowed by the project.

The surface of the competition is generally like a gym floor, but it may be inconsistent when compared to our whiteboard arenas. The competition floor is likely different than the floor when you practice with. All of that could affect the behavior of your robot.

Also, our arena surfaces typically have a 1/8 inch drop to the floor. This should be similar to what students see in class, but your robot will need to traverse that. This could be a problem for robots with a "plow" that scrapes the surface or otherwise has low-hanging parts.

At times during the competition there may be several people, judges or other materials around the arena. We'd like to allow creative solutions that leave the arena, but we need to limit chaos and interference that results from the robots driving around. Its also hard to tell if a robot is out of control or purposely driving away.

When a project has walls, typically at the competition there will be another arena surface an team competing. We do not want teams competing near each other to interfere with each other.

5 Arenas and Project Materials

Rule Notes/Why

The arenas that we use at the final competition are not (for the most part) new. They often have faint pencil marks or other minor blemishes from previous competitions. The arena walls are not perfectly smooth (the lumber may contain knots).

We do avoid using any arena which has sufficient blemishes that we deem it would interfere with the solution to the project. Students should be tolerant of slight blemishes on the surface like light pencil marks. Most robots have no problem tolerating these, but if your students have tolerances so tight that light pencil marks interfere with the functioning of the robot, we ask that you encourage them to loosen their tolerances.

 We reuse materials for cost reasons (as we encourage schools to do). This is not to say the arenas at the competition are poor quality, just that they're not perfect/new. Its also important to not the arenas at the competition are not exactly the same as the onces you used in class.

No one may not touch arena materials during a run.

Stopping a ball from rolling, removing an item could make the robot's job easier, or give an unfair scoring advantage to a team.
Project materials may not be damaged on purpose. Robots must not be built or coded to damage materials. So we can reuse parts for all teams, and not interrupt the competition flow and finish on time.

Many projects depend on the position of the robot, or items on the board being at a certain position to be scored. Cases include, but are not limited to:

  • The robot or an item being "in" or "within" a certain area.
  • The robot or item crosses/enters/leaves a boundary.

Some projects require a robot or some item the robot interacts with to be at a specific place or stop at a specific place by the end of a run. To determine if this has been accomplished, the judge will look straight down from above the arena and ensure the item or robot is over/inside/past the specified boundary.

Unless the project says otherwise, a robot stopping in a certain position is based on the position of the robot's Intelligent brick only, not the rest of the body of the robot.

Projects where the body is considered legal for the stop rule is similar to the rule for the robot fitting within the bounds of a US Letter piece of paper at the beginning of a run for some projects. The "wire squishing" guidance also applies here. Also, the LEGO parts of the body of the robot must still be attached to the robot's Intelligent brick, unless stated otherwise in the project.

Avoid ambiguity and disallow (or allow) certain clever solutions that would make it too easy to achieve the related rule or score.

What it means for an item (e.g. PVC peg, domino) to be considered "knocked over". This is easy if the peg is completely on its side, on the arena surface, or on the floor off of the arena surface. However the peg could fall on a wall, robot, or other materials in the arena. Imagine if the item the peg is in contact with disappeared, would the peg fall over on its own and make full contact with the arena floor? Hopefully this is clear, or a judge will make a ruling.

 This makes projects easier to solve, so we want everyone to face the same challenges and make the competition fair. We want to encourage creative builds and programming solutions.
Objects might still be moving after the team has declared a stop to their run. Judges will not score the run until the object either stop, or come to some state where the scoring is less ambiguous at the judge's discretion. Judges typically won't be able to catch the state of all of the scored objects at exactly the time the students stop the run. We don't have "instant replay". The judge is also usually operating a stopwatch to time the the run. And the state of the objects upon stop is something that teams should consider in how their solution works.
Teams may not move items on the arena before they start a run. Once the robot starts, it may move anything on the arena that can be moved. Scoring rules in the project will tell you what you should or should not move. May give teams an advantage. We want teams to be solving similar problems.

6 Competition requirements

Requirements before you compete. Materials your team must bring.

Rule Notes/Why
Please return your signed release forms at the competition! These forms may be returned any time up to the start of judging. It may be easiest for you to collect the forms and hand them in when you sign in for the final competition. students participating in the competition must submit a signed release form. Also Note: Because Rochester Public Education is mentioned on the release forms some schools have been confused as to whether their students need to sign the release forms. Rochester Public Education is a co-sponsor of the final competition, which is why their name appears next to IBM's name on the release form. All students, whether they are from a school in the Rochester Public Education system or not, need to submit a signed release form. The local media, hosts, Participants will all be taking photographs or video at the event. Photograph release forms ensure that can be done freely to support the program.
If you are planning on bringing a practice arena to the final competition, do not transport the arena on a school bus. You may get a ticket for unsecured cargo and be forced to do some quick thinking to avoid having to leave your arena on the side of the road. This has actually happened to a school in the ILEO program.

Participants in the Advanced final project will place their robots in the designated garage area. Please do this once you've located your seating area and have unpacked your robot.

After competing, you may take your robot back to your table. You'll need it for the short-term round of the competition.

 Some advanced arena layouts are not revealed until the final competition. We don't want teams to adjust their robot build or program once they've seen the final layout. We also expect advanced students to be prepared when they arrive.
Please remember that we do not allow programming of robots during the long-term competition (3:15-4:00). We ask that students check light levels and make any necessary last-minute adjustments to their robot prior to 3:15. Students should make sure that the final version of their program is loaded on the robot and ready to go, preferably before even arriving at the field house. After 3:15 we will have a "laptops closed" policy. If a team is found to be programming after 3:15 the team will still be allowed to compete, but they will not be eligible to place in the competition. Participants that compete later would have an unfair time advantage to complete or tweak their work.
Note a rule, but a best practice/reminder. Have your students make sure that their robots are fully charged on the day of the competition. Also have them make sure that they have the final version of their program loaded on the robot. Would be unfortunate if a team could not compete to its potential due to lack.
Remind students to be careful with their robots! We have had students drop their robot on the way to the arena to be judged. The competition is run on a very tight schedule, and we usually do not have extra slots to allow a team to compete outside of their assigned time. If a robot is damaged the team may be forced to use some of their competition time to repair the robot. Can ruin a student's experience.
Remind your students to watch the power cables! We have cables running under all the tables to provide power to laptops and kits. These cables are chained together, and every year someone will trip on a cable and accidentally unplug several other tables. Usually, they don't even realize they've done this. If you suddenly lose power, do a quick check to see if your extension cord has become unplugged. Many of the laptops loaned out by IBM have unreliable batteries, so this can be a big problem if it happens during the short-term competition prep time. Unplugging power is disruptive to the teams.

We ask that you be respectful of the RCTC staff and keep the gymnasium as clean as possible. Encourage your students to be courteous. We are going to have a large number of students in the Field House, and we want to keep this a positive experience for everybody.

Relatives/guests are welcome to attend and observe the competition, we only ask that they give room to the competing teams and to judges when spectating. To help with this effort the competition area will be separated from the rest of the floor by fences. We ask that only competing teams (or teams that are about to compete) and their coaches be in the competition area. Relatives may come into the competition area to take photographs, but we ask that they maintain a respectful distance so as not to interfere with the competition.

 Ensure a good experience for all attending the competition.

Students and mentors must understand the full rules of a project and this document. Reach out to the ILEO core team is anything is unclear well before the competition to answer any questions, or for a ruling in case other schools/teams have the same questions. Judges and the ILEO core team have the ultimate say on how rules are handled at the competition.

We try to make rules as clear and unambiguous as we can. But sometime language might fail us might be inadvertently confusing. If there is any confusion, and you interpret things differently in class, we cannot honor such interpretations at the competition to ensure a level playing field.

We also want students to be responsible for read and understand the rules so their solutions conform. Students should ask for mentor help as needed. Mentors should reach out to the core team as needed.

IBMers: Please consider wearing an IBM shirt at the final competition.

Mentors/teachers consider the same for any other organizations that support your program.

 Show your company pride! Networking. Good for photos.

7 Working with judges

Rule Notes/Why
Judges have final say and the recorded scores and times. TODO corrections we make? Due to the number of students at the competition and the number of tasks the judges and core team are performing, we don't have the time to resolve student objects. We do trust our judges to be fair and make a few mistakes as possible.

Students must bring their team name slip from the packet distributed on check-in to the competition. Mentors must pass these out and remind students to bring them.

This slip tells students what time and arena they compete. It helps judges ensure the scoring is performed correctly.

Certain projects (typically short-term) the judge will give the team a slip that indicates the students will compete.

Judges would appreciate student help in resetting arenas for the next run. Students must wait for permission from the judge first. The reset must be performed per the project rules. The judge may hold the next run until a judge or student correct the issue.

This speeds up the reset time, possibly giving students more time to perform their runs. But we want the reset to be performed fairly. Also, the judge needs a few moments to correctly score the run before the arena is reset and the next run can start.

During a run, a robot may malfunction or parts may otherwise disconnect. After the run is scored, and before the next run begins, students may reassemble the robot. Time will continue running for timed projects. For non-timed projects, this reassembly must be done in a timely manner.

We want robots to be reassembled so the team can be successful, but we need to limit the time involved to keep the competition as a whole moving.

Laptops, tablets or other programming devices may not be brought to the judging arenas. I.e. students may not change their programs while competing.

Students may encounter things they didn't plan for, or they might not have been prepared. The time it takes to change the program, and test the robot will simply take too much time.

For certain projects, knowledge of the arena layout at the project could give student and advantage (typically for the Advanced division).

Judges can provide students information on their final score, but only the scoring official knows the complete results of all of the judging. The judges and ILEO core team do not provide complete competition results to teams upon completion of the competition, other than the top final rankings in each division.

We don't have the resources to provide scoring or ranking details to all of the teams. What the students learn and their experience is more important than their final ranking.
